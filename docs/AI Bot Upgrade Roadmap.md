# **Advanced AI/ML Architecture and Reinforcement Learning Upgrades for Slovenian Tarok**

The transition of artificial intelligence in imperfect-information extensive-form games (IIEFGs) from baseline heuristic mastery to superhuman, unexploitable performance necessitates a fundamental departure from standard on-policy reinforcement learning paradigms. The current architecture of the Slovenian Tarok bot—utilizing Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), Fictitious Self-Play (FSP), and an Oracle Critic within a Perfect Training, Imperfect Execution (PTIE) framework—establishes a highly competent foundation that successfully navigates the multi-phase structure of the game.1 The integration of a high-throughput Rust engine to drive Python-based training loops enables the generation of thousands of games per second, making advanced, computationally intensive Monte Carlo approaches feasible.1 However, relying solely on PPO and multi-decision head Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) imposes strict theoretical ceilings on the agent's ability to approximate a Nash equilibrium, effectively adapt to the shifting adversarial alliances inherent in Tarok, and dynamically revise beliefs during real-time execution.1  
This comprehensive analysis details a highly technical roadmap for upgrading the existing Tarok AI framework. By bypassing foundational game mechanics to focus strictly on state-of-the-art developments in deep reinforcement learning, causal sequence modeling, recursive belief-based search, and adversarial team game (ATG) subgame solving, the proposed upgrades are designed to propel the current agent to world-champion caliber.3 The analysis synthesizes methodologies derived from milestone systems such as Suphx (Mahjong), Pluribus (Poker), ReBeL, and DTCard (trick-taking games) to provide an actionable blueprint for architectural evolution.

## **Diagnosing the Limitations of the Baseline Architecture**

The existing training lab pipeline employs a structured three-phase approach: Imitation Pre-training via Behavioral Cloning to overcome sparse rewards, PPO Fine-tuning against heuristic bots utilizing plateau monitoring, and Fictitious Self-Play against historical frozen checkpoints to prevent catastrophic forgetting.1 While robust, this framework exhibits several structural and theoretical limitations when measured against the frontiers of multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL).  
Proximal Policy Optimization is an on-policy gradient algorithm designed to maximize expected cumulative rewards in Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) where the full state is observable.1 In imperfect-information games like Tarok, the partial observability violates the Markov property, meaning the optimal action depends not just on the current visible state, but on the entire history of play and the hidden probability distributions of the opponents' cards.7 Standard policy gradient methods, even when augmented with FSP, frequently fail to converge to an exact Nash equilibrium in such environments.2 They tend to produce policies that, while strong against standard baselines, remain exploitable by highly sophisticated opponents capable of identifying deterministic patterns.2 Fictitious Self-Play mitigates cyclic strategies and stabilizes learning by sampling opponents from a pool of past checkpoints, but it does not offer the rigorous bounds on exploitability provided by Counterfactual Regret Minimization (CFR) variants.1  
Furthermore, the current architecture relies on a shared neural trunk with specialized output heads for discrete phases (Bidding, King Calling, Talon Selection, Card Play).1 While this forces a unified latent representation, purely value-based MLP methods struggle to maintain temporal context across the episodic, multi-phase structure of a full Tarok hand without relying on heavily engineered, hand-crafted state histories.3 Finally, the current agent relies entirely on its offline-trained blueprint strategy during inference. Advanced AI systems derive their superhuman performance from combining offline reinforcement learning with online, real-time search.10 Without the ability to re-solve subgames on the fly based on observed opponent actions, the current Tarok agent cannot dynamically adapt to out-of-distribution plays or exploit specific strategic weaknesses identified during a live session.10

## **Advanced State Representation: The Public Belief State**

The most significant theoretical upgrade required for the Tarok agent is the transition from evaluating raw, discrete histories to operating on Public Belief States (PBS). In 54-card trick-taking games where the optimal action depends heavily on the probabilistic distribution of hidden cards, reasoning over deterministic histories is mathematically insufficient.13  
A Public Belief State formulation transforms an imperfect-information game into a continuous-state perfect-information game.13 The state is defined as a tuple consisting of the public state (the bidding sequence, the talon exchanged, the tricks played face-up on the table) and a belief vector, which represents a probability distribution over all possible private states (the unrevealed hands of the opponents).8 Instead of the actor network learning a policy mapped to a specific history, the agent learns a policy mapped to the PBS. This allows the AI to explicitly factor in the continuous probability that an opponent holds high-value cards, such as the *Škis*, the *Mond*, or a specific King, scaling its aggression or conservatism based on mathematical certainty rather than heuristic guessing.13  
To maintain the PBS, the agent must perform Bayesian belief updates as the game progresses.13 At the beginning of a hand, the belief distribution over the opponents' private cards is uniform, conditioned only on the cards currently held by the agent and the unrevealed talon. When an opponent takes an action—such as executing a highly aggressive bid like a "Solo" or discarding a specific suit during play—the agent updates its belief distribution regarding the opponent's private cards.13 To avoid the intractable computational overhead of updating the full joint probability distribution of all remaining cards in the 54-card deck, the agent must utilize factorized likelihood terms for each private feature. These terms are updated recursively using the probability of the observed action sequence given the feature and the historical PBS. This recursive Bayesian update serves as a computational "theory of mind," allowing the neural network to approximate the hidden intentions and constraints of the opponents directly from the trajectory of their public actions.16

## **Next-Generation Neural Network Architectures**

Processing the intractably large state space of a multi-phase trick-taking game requires overhauling the neural network architecture. The transition involves moving away from standard MLPs toward either Decision Transformers designed for causal sequence modeling or Deep Convolutional Neural Networks utilizing highly engineered look-ahead features.3

### **Causal Sequence Modeling via Decision Transformers**

Recent advances demonstrate that trick-taking card games can be elegantly reframed as conditional sequence modeling tasks, an approach formalized in frameworks like DTCard.3 By utilizing a causal Decision Transformer, the agent inherently captures long-range temporal dependencies and implicitly tracks hidden information without requiring the explicit construction of recurrent memory modules or complex search trees.3 Furthermore, sequence modeling provides a stable learning paradigm that circumvents the severe instability of online Temporal Difference (TD) bootstrapping, which is notoriously problematic in sparse-reward, penalty-avoidance environments typical of trick-taking games.3  
The sequence model processes interleaved tokens representing the trajectory of the game. The input layout follows a standard deterministic sequence: return-to-go, observation, and action.3 The observations and returns-to-go are processed via linear projections, while actions and timesteps utilize embedding lookups.3 The network utilizes an autoregressive policy with causal masking to prevent future token leakage, outputting logits over the 54 possible card actions, alongside specialized action spaces for bidding and calling.3  
A critical hyperparameter in this architecture is the context length. Empirical ablation studies on trick-taking games reveal that training stability and performance consistency peak when the context length matches the natural boundary of a single game hand.3 For Tarok, the context window should be strictly bounded to the maximum number of tricks (typically 12\) plus the initial bidding tokens, King calling tokens, and talon exchange tokens. Extending the context window across multiple episodes to encompass a full session introduces severe cross-episodic noise, leading to degraded performance and increased variance.3  
The loss formulation for the Decision Transformer minimizes cross-entropy on action targets over non-padding tokens. A profound advantage of this architecture is its capacity for implicit rule internalization.3 The self-attention mechanism learns the complex, structured legality constraints of Tarok—such as strict follow-suit mechanics and mandatory trumping rules—directly from the data distribution.3 This eliminates the need for strict algorithmic action masking during the training phase, allowing the model to smoothly approximate the conditional probability of legal moves, though lightweight heuristic filtering remains necessary during live inference to ensure absolute compliance with game rules.3  
Extensive ablation studies on dataset sizes for offline pretraining of sequence models in card games reveal a logarithmic dependence between the size of the dataset and the performance of the agent up to a critical threshold.3 Performance saturates across all dataset types once the size exceeds approximately 200,000 game trajectories.3 At this plateau, the architecture reaches a structural performance ceiling, having exhausted the actionable information available in the offline data.3 Interestingly, scaling the data beyond this optimal threshold actually leads to a noticeable degradation in win rates due to the narrow behavioral distributions of heuristic agents used for data generation. The sequence model begins to over-commit to these rigid deterministic patterns, losing its ability to generalize when encountering out-of-distribution states during live evaluation.3 To mitigate this, the optimal dataset composition requires a hybrid 80/20 split: 80 percent rule-based trajectories to provide coherent strategic patterns, and 20 percent random trajectories to inject state-space diversity and prevent overfitting.3

### **Deep Convolutional Architectures and the Suphx Paradigm**

If spatial feature representation and highly parameterized value estimation are preferred over autoregressive sequence modeling, the architecture must transition to Deep Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), following the methodology established by the Suphx AI for Mahjong.6 This involves designing action-specific models (one for bidding, one for card play) using multi-layer CNNs that treat the card game state similarly to a multidimensional image.17  
A competitive CNN architecture for complex imperfect-information games involves four to five convolutional layers followed by a fully-connected layer.17 In designs inspired by Suphx, the first three convolutional layers utilize 64 filters, while the final convolutional layer uses 32 filters.17 All filters share a uniform size, optimized as a hyperparameter for each specific action head. The fully connected layer typically features 256 hidden nodes, utilizing Batch Normalization and Dropout layers to regularize the network and prevent overfitting.17 It is critical not to exceed this depth; expanding the number of convolutional layers or the number of filters per layer in an imperfect-information setting drastically increases the parameter count during the flattening operation.24 This leads to oversized models that exceed GPU memory constraints and bottleneck the high-throughput self-play generation required by the Rust engine.24  
To process the game state through a CNN, the state must be encoded into a multi-channel binary feature matrix.25 The 54-card deck of Tarok cannot be represented as a simple one-dimensional array. Instead, each card is represented as the sum of distinct embeddings, capturing its rank, its suit, and its unique identifier.26 Categorical features, such as the current game phase or the identity of the declarer, are encoded into multiple channels where each channel is populated entirely by zeros or ones.18 Integer features, such as the current session score differential, are partitioned into buckets, with each bucket encoded as a separate binary channel.18  
To maximize the CNN's predictive capability, the input matrix must include engineered "look-ahead features".18 These channels simulate the immediate, deterministic consequences of a potential action. For example, if a specific Tarok card is played, the look-ahead feature evaluates whether that card mathematically guarantees winning the current trick based on the cards already played on the table, encoding this binary truth into a dedicated channel. This relieves the neural network from having to relearn the basic rules of arithmetic and trick resolution, allowing it to focus its capacity on higher-order strategic reasoning and opponent modeling.18 Furthermore, this binary matrix encoding allows the network to seamlessly toggle between the Oracle Critic (which receives perfect information) and the Actor (which operates on imperfect information). To simulate the Actor's perspective, the channels representing the opponents' private cards are simply replaced with tensors of all zeros, ensuring a unified architecture for the Perfect Training, Imperfect Execution (PTIE) paradigm.25

## **Equilibrium Finding: Deep CFR and TurboReBeL**

To achieve unexploitable, world-class play, the core reinforcement learning loop must transition from Proximal Policy Optimization to algorithms within the Counterfactual Regret Minimization (CFR) family, scaled by deep learning capabilities.2 Conventional CFR relies on full game-tree traversals, which limits its scalability and requires extensive expert knowledge to create manual state abstractions.2 Deep Counterfactual Regret Minimization (Deep CFR) obviates the need for manual abstraction by using deep neural networks to intrinsically approximate the behavior of CFR in the full, unabstracted game tree.2

### **Memory Buffers and Advantage Networks**

Deep CFR avoids calculating and accumulating exact regrets at every discrete information set by generalizing across similar states using a Value Network.28 This requires maintaining two massive experience replay buffers to prevent catastrophic forgetting during the training loop.31 The Advantage Memory stores tuples containing the information set features and a vector of sampled instantaneous regrets for each possible action.31 The Policy Memory stores tuples representing the average behavior strategy.31 Because the data generated from a single CFR iteration is highly correlated, Deep CFR must utilize reservoir sampling to maintain a uniform distribution of training data across all iterations.31  
The Value Network, serving as the advantage predictor, is trained using supervised regression to minimize the mean squared error against the sampled regrets. The architecture typically requires an input layer scaled to the 54-card feature matrix, followed by several fully connected layers with ReLU activations, outputting a linear activation for each possible action.31 A significant refinement to this process is Single Deep CFR (SD-CFR), which lowers the overall approximation error by circumventing the need to train a separate average strategy network.2 By relying exclusively on the regret network and adjusting the sampling methodology, SD-CFR demonstrates theoretically lower exploitability and faster convergence in complex card games compared to standard Deep CFR.2

### **The TurboReBeL Acceleration Framework**

While Deep CFR provides a massive leap in capability, the absolute state-of-the-art framework for large-scale IIEFGs is Recursive Belief-based Learning (ReBeL), which successfully integrates self-play reinforcement learning with depth-limited search.4 ReBeL extends the AlphaZero paradigm to imperfect-information settings by operating exclusively on the Public Belief State.13 During execution, ReBeL initializes a subgame characterized by the PBS of its root node. It then runs CFR for a specified number of iterations ($T$) to approximate the Nash equilibrium within this bounded subgame.26 At the conclusion of these iterations, the root PBS and its calculated value label are appended to the training dataset for the Value Network.26 This approach is vastly superior to Fictitious Self-Play because it grounds its value estimations in rigorous equilibrium approximations rather than moving-target empirical averages generated by historical checkpoints.  
However, the primary limitation of ReBeL is its profound computational cost. Evaluating each PBS sample requires $T$ full iterations of CFR, often demanding billions of samples and millions of GPU hours to achieve convergence in games of comparable complexity to Tarok.4 To make this algorithm viable within the constraints of the Tarok Training Lab, the architecture must implement the TurboReBeL refinements.4  
TurboReBeL achieves a remarkable 250-fold acceleration in training speed.4 It accomplishes this via Single-Sample Multi-Iteration Generation. Rather than executing separate, computationally expensive sampling passes for every CFR iteration, the framework fixes the subgame strategies to CFR-averaged policies and generates the data for all $T$ iterations in a single sampling pass.4 This core innovation yields a theoretical $O(T)$ speedup, reducing the total compute cost to a fraction of a percent of standard ReBeL while perfectly matching its low exploitability metrics.4  
To further reduce the sample complexity required to comprehensively cover the PBS state space, TurboReBeL employs Isomorphic Data Augmentation.4 This technique leverages game-theoretic invariants to artificially multiply the dataset without introducing bias. In the context of Tarok, this takes the form of Suit Isomorphism. If the agent encounters a specific PBS where certain non-trump suits are strategically equivalent (e.g., neither hearts nor spades are trump, and both suits have identical outstanding card distributions based on the public history), the state-value data can be cloned and swapped across the suits.4 Because the TurboReBeL augmentation operates directly on the inputs to the PBS value network rather than within the action policy, it dramatically accelerates convergence in the early training phases with minimal computational overhead.4

## **Adversarial Team Games and Safe Subgame Solving**

Slovenian Tarok is fundamentally defined by its bidding phase, which routinely dictates asymmetric cooperative dynamics for the subsequent trick-taking phase. Depending on the contract won (e.g., calling a specific King versus playing a Solo), the game creates shifting 1v2 or 1v3 configurations, pitting the Declarer against a temporary alliance of Defenders. This structural reality categorizes the trick-taking phase as an Adversarial Team Game (ATG).6 In an ATG, multiple players coordinate as a team to maximize a shared utility against a single adversary. The profound technical challenge arises from the defenders' inability to communicate their private cards to one another during play, necessitating decentralized coordination under severe asymmetric partial information.6  
Relying solely on an offline blueprint strategy—even one trained via TurboReBeL or Decision Transformers—is demonstrably insufficient for superhuman performance in ATGs. The agent must implement online, real-time strategy refinement, a technique broadly categorized as Safe Subgame Solving.10 In perfect-information games, determining the optimal strategy at a decision point only requires knowledge of the subgame rooted at that exact node.10 In imperfect-information games, the exact node is unknown, and the optimal strategy in a subgame heavily depends on the strategies employed in unreached portions of the broader game tree.10 Subgame solving overcomes this by using the offline blueprint to approximate a solution for the whole game, and then improving that solution for specific, localized subgames during live execution.10

### **The Team Belief DAG and Gadget Game Generation**

To perform mathematically rigorous subgame solving in an ATG, the framework must utilize a Team Belief Directed Acyclic Graph (TB-DAG).6 The TB-DAG provides a structural representation of the decision problem and strategy space of a team operating under asymmetric information. The graph contains observation nodes and decision points defined as beliefs. A team belief node identifies a subset of game histories that are functionally indistinguishable to the team based on the public information available.6 Edges leaving a belief node correspond to prescriptions—tuples that assign a specific action to every information set intersecting that belief.6 Following a prescription transitions the graph to an observation node where the team receives new public information (such as an opponent playing a card), leading directly to the next decision belief.6 Despite its worst-case exponential size relative to information complexity, the TB-DAG is highly sparse when dealing with pure strategies, allowing the agent to restrict its computations to plausible game trajectories.6  
When the Tarok agent encounters a specific public state during live play, it halts the execution of its static blueprint and re-optimizes its strategy exclusively for the subgame rooted at that specific state. The "trunk"—the history of play leading up to the current state—remains fixed.6 To compute the refined strategy without violating the theoretical bounds of the Nash equilibrium, an auxiliary construction known as a Gadget Game (or Gadget DAG) is instantiated.6 The gadget game is generated from the subgame of the TB-DAG that is played with strictly positive probability in the blueprint strategy.6 It adds a synthetic root node that grants the opponent the option to choose any information set within the public state to restart the subgame, effectively challenging the refining agent to defend against the absolute worst-case scenario.6

### **The Team-Maxmargin Formulation and Column-Generation**

The core mathematical logic for refining the strategy within the gadget game relies on the Team-maxmargin linear program. This optimization is formulated to maximize the margin—defined as the difference between the expected value of the subgame and the value of the counterfactual best response to the team's blueprint strategy.6  
The objective function maximizes this margin as follows:

$$\\max\_{x'} \\min\_{y, \\bar{y}} \\sum\_{z \\succeq P} x'\[z\] y\[z\] p\[z\] u\[z\] \- \\sum\_{C \\in \\mathcal{C}\[x, P\]} y\[C\] u\[x, C\]$$  
where $x'$ represents the refined strategy being computed, $y$ and $\\bar{y}$ denote the opponent's potential deviations, $p\[z\]$ is the nature reach probability, and $u\[z\]$ is the terminal utility.6 The trunk strategy of the team is rigidly constrained, ensuring that the refined strategy does not alter decisions made in the past.  
A crucial, mathematically vital adjustment must occur at the belief nodes via payoff and reach normalization. The algorithm enforces the specific constraint $y\[C\] \= \\bar{y}\[C\] / \\rho\[x, C\]$ for all opponent beliefs $C$.6 This rescales the opponent's reach probabilities according to the counterfactual reach $\\rho\[x, C\]$. This normalization is absolutely necessary in team games because it accounts for fixed reach probabilities in the trunk. Unlike 1v1 zero-sum games where normalization typically occurs at the terminal nodes, ATGs require normalization directly at the belief nodes because the reach of the defending team relies directly on the deviation chosen by the opponent.6  
Because expanding the full TB-DAG in real-time is computationally impossible during a standard Tarok turn, the agent utilizes a Column-Generation (CG) algorithm to solve the gadget game.6 Instead of mapping the entire graph, the CG algorithm iteratively enlarges a highly restricted set of strategies by employing a Best-Response Oracle (BRO).6 The memory footprint of the strategy representation grows only linearly with the number of BRO calls, allowing the agent to refine its strategy in an "anytime" fashion—dynamically halting the computation and outputting the best available strategy when the allocated thinking time limit expires.6 Empirical data indicates that subgame solving using these CG techniques in team games can reduce the exploitability gap by roughly 39 percent after just a single move, providing a massive advantage over static blueprint execution.6

## **Algorithmic Refinements for Offline-to-Online Transfer**

To finalize the world-class capabilities of the Tarok agent, the training loop must incorporate highly specialized algorithmic refinements designed to bridge the gap between offline training and live, real-time execution. These techniques ensure the agent maximizes the utility of its immediate observations while maintaining long-horizon strategic coherence.

### **Parametric Monte Carlo Policy Adaptation (pMCPA)**

Pioneered by the Suphx system in Mahjong, Parametric Monte Carlo Policy Adaptation (pMCPA) allows the agent to dynamically adapt its generalized baseline policy to the specific, highly idiosyncratic cards drawn in the current hand.12 At the beginning of a hand—specifically after the talon is dealt and the bidding phase has concluded, but before the first trick is played—the agent executes rapid self-play rollouts. These rollouts use its actual private cards against simulated opponent hands drawn from the initial Public Belief State.12  
The agent applies standard policy gradient updates to a temporary, cloned instance of its neural network based on the outcomes of these simulated rollouts.6 It then uses this temporarily adapted, overfitted policy to play the actual hand. Once the hand concludes, the adapted weights are purged from memory, and the agent reverts back to the global blueprint for the next hand.12 This real-time overfitting ensures the agent extracts the maximum mathematical expectation from its specific starting hand, bypassing the generalized averaging of the baseline network that occurs during offline training.

### **Global Reward Prediction and Session Maximization**

The current Tarok Training Lab successfully optimizes for session-based score maximization across multi-game episodes (e.g., maximizing the cumulative score differential over a 20-game set rather than simply optimizing for single-game win rates).1 To vastly improve the neural network's ability to navigate this exceptionally long-horizon objective, a Global Reward Prediction framework must be implemented.36  
This involves deploying a dedicated recurrent neural network, such as a two-layer Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) followed by fully connected layers, to act as a long-term reward predictor.12 Early in the hand—such as during the highly volatile bidding phase—this recurrent network predicts the final score differential of the entire session based on the current trajectory.12 This predicted global reward is then fed as an auxiliary input feature into the main policy network. By providing the actor network with a probabilistic "glimpse" into the likely macro-outcome of the session, the agent learns vastly superior risk management. For example, the agent learns to mathematically evaluate whether it should conservatively defend a weak hand to preserve a massive session lead, or aggressively bid a high-variance contract to overcome a severe point deficit.38

### **Oracle Guiding and Distillation**

The current architecture utilizes an Oracle Critic that receives perfect information during training, part of the Perfect Training, Imperfect Execution (PTIE) paradigm.1 This mechanism can be dramatically enhanced by implementing Oracle Guiding, a technique that tightens the relationship between the omniscient critic and the blinded actor.12  
In standard PTIE, the critic evaluates the state value using perfect information, while the actor learns an imperfect-information policy based on the critic's value assessments. Oracle Guiding introduces an auxiliary distillation loss during the self-play reinforcement learning phase. The hidden information—such as the exact distribution of the unplayed Taroks across the opponents' hands—is used to guide the direction of the actor's representation layer. This forces the actor's latent space to closely mimic the oracle's latent space.38 By minimizing the distance between the representations, the actor network profoundly enhances its ability to intuitively deduce the hidden state purely from visible public information, achieving a highly accurate artificial theory of mind.38

### **Selective Expert Iteration (SEI)**

If the causal sequence modeling architecture (Decision Transformer) is adopted, the offline-to-online transition must be fiercely protected against distribution collapse.3 The current plateau monitoring technique, which halts training based on win-rate stabilization against heuristic bots, is insufficient for preventing the performance degradation that occurs when models overfit to their own generated data during self-play.1  
Selective Expert Iteration (SEI) provides a strict, mathematically grounded acceptance-filtering mechanism.3 As the agent generates new trajectories during Fictitious Self-Play or TurboReBeL rollouts, these trajectories are not immediately appended to the replay buffer. Instead, they are subjected to an inference-time heuristic filter. Only trajectories that rank in the upper percentiles of cumulative reward, or demonstrate exceptionally high conditional coherence based on the Oracle Critic's evaluation, are marked as "Accepted".3 The model then self-trains exclusively on these filtered, high-quality trajectories. This monotonic refinement stabilizes the learning process and prevents the sequence model from over-committing to the rigid, deterministic patterns that frequently arise in unsupervised self-play, ensuring the agent remains highly dynamic and unexploitable during live evaluation.3

## **Synthesis and Strategic Deployment**

To elevate the Slovenian Tarok bot to a world-champion, mathematically unexploitable state, incremental hyperparameter tuning of the current PPO and FSP architecture is fundamentally inadequate. The framework must undergo a structural and theoretical transition toward exact equilibrium-finding algorithms and real-time subgame search.  
The integration of a Decision Transformer optimized with a bounding context length provides a robust alternative to unstable temporal difference bootstrapping, allowing the agent to implicitly internalize the complex legality constraints of Tarok without extensive hand-engineering. The transition from raw historical arrays to the Public Belief State, managed through recursive Bayesian factored likelihoods, allows the agent to reason probabilistically about the distribution of critical cards like the *Škis* and *Mond*.  
Replacing Proximal Policy Optimization with the TurboReBeL framework, augmented by Single-Sample Multi-Iteration Generation and Suit Isomorphism, guarantees the computation of robust Nash equilibrium approximations while circumventing the prohibitive GPU compute costs typically associated with depth-limited search in imperfect information games. Finally, the deployment of Team Belief DAGs, Gadget Game generation, and Column-Generation algorithms enables real-time Safe Subgame Solving. This addresses the core strategic complexity of Tarok—the shifting 1v2 and 1v3 Adversarial Team Game dynamics—allowing the agent to dynamically adapt to opponent deviations and refine its strategy on the fly.  
By systematically implementing this synthesis of causal sequence modeling, recursive belief-based learning, and adversarial team game subgame solving, the resulting architecture will reliably produce strategies that transcend heuristic baselines, converging upon an unexploitable, state-of-the-art intelligence capable of mastering the profound complexities of Slovenian Tarok.
